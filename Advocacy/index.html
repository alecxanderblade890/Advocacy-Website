FITNESS PLAN

Assess Your Fitness Level

You're undoubtedly aware of your level of fitness. However, measuring and documenting your baseline fitness scores can provide you with standards against which to measure your improvement.
Consider keeping track of the following to measure your aerobic and muscular fitness, flexibility, and body composition:
1. Your pulse rate before and after walking one mile (1.6 kilometers).
2. How long does it take to walk one mile or how long does it take to run 1.5 miles? (2.41 kilometers).
3. How many conventional or modified pushups can you perform at once?
4. How far forward you can reach while sitting on the floor with your legs in front of you.
5. The circumference of your waist, right above your hipbones.
6. Your BMI (body mass index).

Design Your Fitness Program

1. Consider your fitness objectives. Are you embarking on a fitness regimen to help you lose weight? Or are you motivated by something else, such as training for a marathon? Having specific objectives might help you track your progress and stay motivated.
2. Create a balanced routine. For most healthy adults, the Department of Health and Human Services recommends getting at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity or 75 minutes of vigorous aerobic activity a week, or a combination of moderate and vigorous activity. The guidelines suggest that you spread out this exercise during the course of a week. Greater amounts of exercise will provide even greater health benefits.
3. Start low and progress slowly. If you're just beginning to exercise, start cautiously and progress slowly. If you have an injury or a medical condition, consult your doctor or an exercise therapist for help designing a fitness program that gradually improves your range of motion, strength and endurance.
4. Build activity into your daily routine. Finding time to exercise can be a challenge. To make it easier, schedule time to exercise as you would any other appointment. Plan to watch your favorite show while walking on the treadmill, read while riding a stationary bike, or take a break to go on a walk at work.
5. Allow time for recovery. Many people start exercising with frenzied zeal — working out too long or too intensely — and give up when their muscles and joints become sore or injured. Plan time between sessions for your body to rest and recover.

Assemble Your Equipment

If you're going to buy workout equipment, be sure it's functional, pleasant, and simple to use. Before investing in your own equipment, you may want to test out several types of equipment at a fitness club. Consider fitness applications for smart devices or other activity monitoring gadgets, such as those that can track your mileage, calories burnt, or heart rate.

Get Started

1. Begin cautiously and progressively increase your workload. Allow enough time to warm up and cool down with moderate walking or mild stretching. Then accelerate to a pace that you can maintain for five to ten minutes without becoming fatigued. Increase the amount of time you workout progressively as your stamina increases. Increase your exercise time to 30 to 60 minutes most days of the week.
2. If necessary, break things up. You don't have to perform all of your workout at once, so spread it out throughout the day. Shorter but more frequent workouts provide similar aerobic effects. Exercising in small bursts many times a day may work better for your schedule than a single 30-minute session. Any level of exercise is preferable to none at all.
3. Pay attention to your body. Take a pause if you experience discomfort, shortness of breath, dizziness, or nausea. You could be overworking yourself.
4. Be adaptable. Give yourself permission to take a day or two off if you're not feeling well.

Monitor Your Progress

Take your personal fitness exam again six weeks after you begin your program, and then every few months after that. You may find that in order to continue improving, you need to increase the amount of time you exercise. Or you could be pleasantly pleased to discover that you're exercising just enough to fulfill your fitness objectives. Set new objectives or attempt a new hobby if you lose motivation. Exercising with a buddy or attending a fitness facility class may also be beneficial. Getting started with a fitness regimen is a big step. But it doesn't have to be a huge one. By properly planning and timing yourself, you may create a healthy habit that will last a lifetime.

Personal fitness program

Develop your own fitness program fit for your goals you want to achieve. Determine your fitness level before setting new goals. It is a good idea to set fitness objectives for yourself that you can achieve throughout your life in order to maintain your fitness level. The more you can set new objectives for yourself and your body, the more likely you are to follow through on them. Some individuals find that competition motivates them to exercise, so you might want to check into local races or charity walks to help keep you motivated to exercise. You will have a single objective in mind while training for these events, which will help you stay on track.
